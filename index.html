Tutorial by Madison Powers

Motivation for this project/topic: The issue of missing persons is a signifigant and important social phenomenon.

And others go missing due to accidents(hiking, drownings). Missing persons with a crimial element often include: kidnappings, child abductions, sex trafficing, murder, etc.

Another reason I wanted to explore missing persons data is because often times these cases are overlooked by the general public. Since we've only been able to recently explore this data in any depth, many opportunities remain to have signifigant impact on understanding the issue and bringing people home.

Data Wrangling and Cleaning¶

For the purpose of this project, I will be dropping some columns of data that I will not be using. This does not mean that this data is not important, just that it is out of the scope of this project. For example, circumstance data would be really useful to analyze, possibly through natural language processing. But, once again, for the purpose of this project, I will be ommiting it. Additonally, I will be dropping: aged photo, photo, org contact, country and state. Once again, the information in these columns are extremely useful in other applications, but are out of the scope of this project. (Note: I am dropping both country and state since all of this data is for California)

The dataset is pretty clean, but there are some missing values. Some of the colums that have missing data are: middle name, namus_number, ncmec_number, county, and city. Middle names can be missing since some people don't have them or it isn't in the missing persons report. Namus number and ncmec number will be missing due to the missing person being from one database and not the other. County and city are missing for some people as well. An easy way to 'fix' this is by filling any empty strings with not available(NA)s. This will make processing easier, but this may lead to some bias when doing calculations with the data. Similar to numpy's not a number(NaN), pandas Not available will be treated as a 0 in calculations.

Data Exploration Part 2¶

In this second part, I wanted to explore how age relates to number of missing persons. Do teens go missing more? Do senior citizens go missing more? Is there even a relationship between age and missing persons? These are some of the questions that come to mind.

According to NCMEC, in 2020, 91% of missing childrens cases were endangered runaways(https://www.missingkids.org/footer/media/keyfacts). From this, I would expect, that the number of missing teen/young adults would be larger than the number of missing children(0-12).

In order to organize this data for visualization, I will be using pandas.cut to create bins. When binning data, you can create bins with the same number of data or bins with the same range of data. For the purpose of age, I will be splitting the data into 4 bins: children(0-12), teens/young adults(12-20), adults(20-65), and seniors(65+). The reason for this disctinction is to focus on the 'main' groups of people. You could group people differently, and you might gain some new insight. Additionally, the teens/young adults bin ends at 20 because NCMEC includes people 20 and younger. See: https://www.missingkids.org/content/dam/missingkids/pdfs/publications/nc74.pdf (pg 37 of pdf/ Missing and Abducted Children - 19)
